Personal data is broadcast daily to invasive companies and governments, eroding everyday privacy. Data gathering has become a substantial business — some firms collect information for their own marketing, while others sell it directly to the highest bidder. Anyone who has read the permissions granted to a typical smartphone app has seen this firsthand: contacts, email addresses, camera and microphone access are routinely handed over because the perceived convenience outweighs privacy concerns. The result is relentless targeted advertising and, more concerningly, the potential for elections and public opinion to be subtly influenced without awareness.

The countermeasure is to disallow collection of personal information in the first place, which requires adopting alternative technologies. Blockchain — originally implemented as Bitcoin in 2009 — provides a decentralized, algorithmic platform operated by computers worldwide. It is open-source, transparent, and built on strong encryption. Since Bitcoin's inception, the underlying technology has advanced through alternative coins and code modifications, expanding well beyond digital currency into broader applications.

Digital currency itself inherits the potential to replace parts of the current monetary system. It operates without government regulation, runs as a decentralized system with no single point of failure, and is spent, traded, and exchanged for fiat globally. Bitcoin's price history has been volatile but demonstrably valuable; it is recognized as a taxable currency in many countries. Regardless of price speculation, the underlying blockchain technology is what retains lasting value and will drive new business solutions.
