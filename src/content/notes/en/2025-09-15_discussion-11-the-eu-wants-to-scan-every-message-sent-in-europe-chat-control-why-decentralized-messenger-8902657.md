In *The Guardian* article "The EU wants to scan every message sent in Europe. Will that really make us safer?" Apostolos Fotiadis examines the EU's proposal to introduce large-scale digital surveillance under the banner of protecting children from sexual abuse material (CSAM). The proposal would require all messages sent across digital platforms — including end-to-end encrypted chats — to be scanned for CSAM. Making such scanning possible would require weakening or bypassing encryption entirely, creating systemic vulnerabilities exploitable not only by authorities but also by malicious actors.

Security experts warn that the system risks eroding privacy, enabling misuse, and expanding surveillance far beyond its stated purpose. Critics also note that the focus is almost entirely on technical surveillance, sidelining broader strategies such as education, awareness, and social services. The law remains stalled, with several EU member states concerned about the impact on cybersecurity.

While protecting children is a vital goal, the proposed measures threaten to undermine fundamental rights to privacy and security. A balance must be found that protects children without turning all personal communication into potential evidence.

## Why Decentralized Messengers Could Be the Future

Current trends suggest decentralized messengers may form the backbone of secure communication going forward. Decentralized systems often lack a central authority that can be compelled to insert backdoors or weaken encryption. Keys remain user-controlled, making mass surveillance technically harder. Without a single choke point, governments and corporations have less ability to monitor, censor, or shut down communication; users can choose trusted nodes while end-to-end encryption remains intact.

Centralized entities can be pressured to change policies or compromise security. Decentralized networks distribute influence and reduce the risk of unilateral abuse. Open, community-driven projects also tend to experiment with new approaches, leading to more resilient, user-centric solutions. Hybrid models may emerge, combining decentralization with selective centralized services.

## Challenges

Even decentralized systems face regulatory crackdowns, with authorities potentially targeting node operators. Technical hurdles remain around scalability, strong spam protections, and usability — all requiring careful design. Mainstream users are accustomed to polished, integrated apps, so decentralized messengers must match that experience to gain meaningful traction.

Decentralized messengers or hybrid architectures may rise as a natural counter to growing surveillance pressure. They are not a perfect solution, but they represent a strong path toward secure, private, and censorship-resistant communication — potentially the foundation of the next era of digital messaging.
